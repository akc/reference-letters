{\bf Reference for Atli Fannar Franklín}
\bigskip

Dear Vincent Delecroix,

I strongly support Atli Fannar Franklín's application for the
postdoctoral position in the CartesEtPlus project at LaBRI. I have
known Atli since he was an undergraduate at the University of Iceland,
where I supervised his BSc project. It was clear even then that he was
a very gifted mathematics student: he presented original work in his
BSc project, and received the Sigurður Helgason award. I have now
supervised his Ph.D., which he defended in April 2026. Atli is a
persistent and technically broad enumerative combinatorialist, with an
unusually computational style, and I recommend him with confidence.

Atli's thesis, ``Enumerating score sequences and permutations by
inversions and forbidden patterns'', collects four papers. The first,
joint with Dukes, Stefánsson, and me in Proceedings of the AMS,
attacks the century-old problem of counting tournament score sequences
(MacMahon, 1920). A factorization of the generating function for
pointed score sequences yields a closed formula and a quadratic-time
algorithm, and along the way settles a 2013 conjecture of Hanna as a
corollary. The second, joint with Steingrímsson and me in the
Electronic Journal of Combinatorics, finds the generating function
$S_0(x)$ for permutations of length $n$ with exactly $n$ inversions
and shows that $(xC(x))^i S_0(x)$, where $C(x)$ is the generating
function for the Catalan numbers, counts those with $n-i$ inversions.
I had put the formula for $S_0(x)$, which was a conjecture at the
time, to several of the best combinatorialists in my circle without
anyone finding a proof. Atli eventually did. The third paper,
single-authored in DMTCS, enumerates indecomposable permutations
avoiding any combination of classical patterns of length at most three
by inversions, via explicit bijections to fountains of coins,
parallelogram polyominoes, and similar objects. The fourth, also
single-authored and currently submitted, encodes 1324-avoiding
permutations as walks in a directed graph. Conditional on an explicit
conjecture, this would yield a new lower bound of $10.418$ on the
growth rate of $\mathrm{Av}(1324)$.

Programming is an essential research tool for Atli. He uses the
computer to probe examples: he accumulates data, identifies the
pattern, and only then commits to a proof. He has applied this approach to all
the papers mentioned above. In particular, the directed-graph
machinery of the fourth paper would be hard to discover without that workflow.

Atli has also built an unusually strong record in teaching and
outreach. He has taught
widely in mathematics and computer science at both the University of
Iceland and Reykjavik University. He has been a long-time
problem author and organiser for both the Icelandic mathematical and
programming Olympiads, currently serves as team leader of the Icelandic
IMO team, and is head of jury for the Nordic Collegiate Programming
Contest.

If I am to flag a limitation, it is that Atli has not yet set his own
research agenda. Once a hard problem is on the
table he will not let go: he trusts it will yield, and he stays with
it long after most would have walked away. But he has yet to develop
the taste for choosing which questions to pursue, and the longer-term
vision to draw them together into a programme of his own. His writing,
too, has its idiosyncrasies: there is a personal voice there, but it
sometimes lacks precision and can be harder to follow than it needs to
be. I am confident he will develop on both fronts in time.

Atli would be an excellent postdoc for the CartesEtPlus project, and I
support his application without reservation. If further information
would be useful, I would be happy to provide it.
