{\bf Reference for Atli Fannar Franklín}
\bigskip

Dear Vincent Delecroix,

I am writing in support of Atli Fannar Franklín's application for the
postdoctoral position in the CartesEtPlus project at LaBRI. I have
known Atli since he was an undergraduate at the University of Iceland.
I supervised his BSc project, which received the Sigurður Helgason
award, and have now supervised his Ph.D., defended in April 2026. He
is a persistent, technically broad, unusually computational enumerative
combinatorialist, and I recommend him with confidence.

Atli's thesis, ``Enumerating score sequences and permutations by
inversions and forbidden patterns'', collects four papers. The first,
joint with Dukes, Stefánsson, and me in Proceedings of the AMS, attacks
the century-old problem of counting tournament score sequences
(MacMahon, 1920): a factorization of the generating function for
pointed score sequences yields a closed formula
and a quadratic-time algorithm, and along the way settles a 2013 OEIS
conjecture of Hanna as a corollary. The second, joint with
Steingrímsson and me in
the Electronic Journal of Combinatorics, finds the generating function
$S_0(x)$ for permutations of length $n$ with exactly $n$ inversions and
shows that $(xC(x))^i S_0(x)$ counts those with $n-i$ inversions; this
was a problem I had put to several of the best combinatorialists in my
circle without finding a solution. The third,
single-authored in DMTCS, enumerates indecomposable permutations
avoiding any combination of classical patterns of length at most three
by inversions, via explicit bijections to fountains of coins,
parallelogram polyominoes, and similar objects. The fourth, also
single-authored and currently submitted, encodes 1324-avoiding
permutations as walks in a directed graph; conditional on an explicit
conjecture, this would yield a new lower bound of $10.418$ on the
growth rate of $\mathrm{Av}(1324)$.

Programming is a genuine research tool for Atli, not just something he
teaches. He uses the computer as a laboratory: he writes the generator
first, accumulates data, identifies the pattern, and only then commits
to a proof. The directed-graph machinery of paper IV would be hard to
discover without that workflow.

Atli's teaching and outreach record is also exceptional. He has taught
widely in mathematics and computer science at both the University of
Iceland and the University of Reykjavik. He has been a long-time
problem author and organiser for both the Icelandic mathematical and
programming Olympiads, currently serves as team leader of the Icelandic
IMO team, and is head of jury of the Nordic Collegiate Programming
Contest.

If I am to flag a limitation, it is that Atli has not yet set his own
research agenda. He pushes hard on hard problems once they are on the
table, but he has yet to develop the taste for choosing which
questions to pursue, and the longer-term vision to draw them together
into a programme of his own. A postdoc in an active group with
concrete projects, such as the CartesEtPlus team in Bordeaux, is
exactly the setting in which to do so.

In short, Atli would be an excellent postdoc for the CartesEtPlus
project, and his application has my strongest support. Please do not
hesitate to contact me if there is anything further I can add.
