{\bf Reference for Atli Fannar Franklín}
\bigskip

Dear Vincent Delecroix,

I am writing in support of Atli Fannar Franklín's application for the
postdoctoral position in the CartesEtPlus project at LaBRI. I have
known Atli since he was an undergraduate at the University of Iceland.
I supervised his BSc project, which received the Sigurður Helgason
award, and have now supervised his Ph.D., defended in April 2026. He
is a persistent, technically broad, unusually computational enumerative
combinatorialist, and I recommend him with confidence.

Atli's thesis, ``Enumerating score sequences and permutations by
inversions and forbidden patterns'', collects four papers. The first,
in Proceedings of the AMS, settles Hanna's long-standing conjecture on
tournament score sequences via a factorization of the generating
function of pointed sequences; it yields a closed formula and a
quadratic-time algorithm, and has already attracted considerable
attention. The second, in the Electronic Journal of Combinatorics,
settles a problem on the subdiagonals of the Mahonian triangle that I
had been circulating among colleagues for years without success; the
factorization reducing every subdiagonal to $S_0(x)$ is particularly
satisfying. The third, single-authored in DMTCS, enumerates
indecomposable permutations avoiding any combination of classical
patterns of length at most three by inversions, via explicit bijections
to fountains of coins, parallelogram polyominoes, and similar objects.
The fourth, also single-authored and currently submitted, encodes
1324-avoiding permutations as walks in a generating-tree-style directed
graph; conditional on an explicit conjecture, this gives a new lower
bound of $10.418$ on the growth rate of $\mathrm{Av}(1324)$.

Programming is a genuine research tool for Atli, not just something he
teaches. He uses the computer as a laboratory: he writes the generator
first, accumulates data, identifies the pattern, and only then commits
to a proof. The directed-graph machinery of paper IV would be hard to
discover without that workflow.

Atli's teaching and outreach record is also exceptional. He has taught
widely in mathematics and computer science at both the University of
Iceland and the University of Reykjavik, and has been a long-time
problem author, organiser, jury member, and team leader in the
Icelandic mathematical and programming Olympiads.

If I am to flag a limitation, it is that Atli has not yet set his own
research agenda. He pushes hard on hard problems and generates ideas
readily once a problem is on the table, but he has not yet developed
the habit of choosing his own line of attack from a blank page. A
postdoc in an active group with concrete projects, such as the
CartesEtPlus team in Bordeaux, is exactly the setting in which to do so.

In short, Atli would be an excellent postdoc for the CartesEtPlus
project, and his application has my strongest support. Please do not
hesitate to contact me if there is anything further I can add.
